Damming in Upstate New York: Exacerbating Natural Disasters?

Hurricane Irene hit upstate New York hard, causing flooding throughout the region, but did local infrastructure contribute to the extent of the flooding?



After a century of the Lock 7 Dam's "fixed" crest impeding runoff drainage and thus [causing] frequently flooding... a strategic 21st century target should be a suitably sized gate-system installed at the Lock 7 dam.

Schenectady Stockade and its history of flooding.

The Stockade district of Schenectady, NY is positioned next to the Mohawk river. In the last century, it has flooded 25 times. Floods are sometimes caused by ice jams in the winter, but often occur after severe storms, like Hurricane Irene. The usual height of the Mohawk river is 212 feet. The flood of 1913 is classified as a "100 year flood" and peaked at 232.9ft.

Effects of Flooding

The National Weather Service classifies major flooding in the Schenectady area when the peak water height is above 224 ft. At that height water enters the basement of homes in the area and critical electricity-producing infrastructure needs to be protected. At 226 ft, the height of the water during Hurricane Irene, water enters the first floor of many homes.

In the last 100 years most floods were classified as "major," and the frequency of flooding has increased over the past few decades.

Context of the Area

During Hurricane Irene, certain low-elevation areas were susceptible to flooding. In response, for newer buildings in the area, landowners have begun to raise the ground level in order to minimize the chance of damage. One such building is the Mohawk Harbor Hotel, which was raised between 6-8ft before building.

Context of the Area

The FEMA flood maps show the 100-year flood areas, or areas in which there is a 1% chance annually that there will be a flood that inundates the area. This is data that they provide to flood insurers in order to establish rates. At some points in Schenectady, the flood area extends over a mile beyond the water's edge.

How can we minimize flooding?

Since the Lock 7 Dam is now over a century old, independent researchers have looked into how the dam may be affecting the Schenectady flood plain. With 100 years of above-average major flood incidence, the area has suffered billions of dollars in damage.

Unlike modern dams, the Lock 7 dam cannot raise or lower its height to modulate water flow. The NYS Canal Corp and NYS Power Authority have ignored researchers' claims that retrofitting the dam would help mitigate flooding. Additionally, they refused to fund a study that would formally assess the potential impact the dam has on the area.

Experiment: Dam Discharge Rate Affects Entire Region

To test the hypothesis that the Lock 7 dam affects flood levels, we look at the river height On September 7th, 2016, when Lock 7's turbines were opened. This allowed water go through dam at a faster rate. The water height is constantly recorded at 3 different places along the river, spaced out across 11 miles. Click the play button to see how Lock 7 affects river heights upstream. Extending this logic, if the dam could let out more water during flood periods, it would reduce flooding.

The dam is a fixed height, unlike other, more modern, dams that use flood gates to let more water pass through during inclement weather

During Hurricane Irene the peak flow rate of the river over the dam was about 130,000 cubic feet per second (cfs). The usual flow rate of the river is between 2,000 and 10,000 cfs. The inundation of that much water in that short amount of time is what caused the flooding.

Conclusion

Upon starting this project, we hoped to illuminate the need for attention to critical infrastructure in the Upstate New York area. The visualizations show that there is a need to retrofit the dam in order to prevent further damage. The New York Power Authority (NYPA) is already in the midst of multi-million dollar projects to improve efficiency on dams including the Lock 7 power plant. Now is the time to advocate for flood protections that the residents of the area deserve.
